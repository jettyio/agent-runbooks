# Strategic Diagnosis — Meridian Health Software

## Executive Read

Meridian is in structural deceleration, not a cyclical growth pause. The core acquisition engine is deteriorating across every dimension (CAC +38%, win rate −8pp, payback 19 months), retention is eroding (NRR 96% down from 112%), and a well-capitalized competitor is attacking from below with a free substitute bundled to dental insurance — a structural cost-of-switching advantage that Meridian cannot price against. The decision before the board is whether to raise a $40M Series B to fund an upmarket move into DSOs, or pursue a higher-leverage path. The single most important implication: both Meridian's own pilot evidence (two DSO pilots churned in six months citing the same specific product gaps) and its 14-month cash runway make the $40M Series B a bet on a hypothesis the company's own data currently refutes. This diagnosis frames the decision as: test the DSO hypothesis with targeted capital and evidence before committing to the larger raise.

### Strategic Issues

1. The core customer segment (independent dental practices) is under structural attack by a well-capitalized free substitute — and Meridian has no demonstrated or evidenced defense against a competitor that has permanently eliminated price as a decision factor.
2. The natural upmarket escape route (DSOs) has already failed twice due to specific, documented, and apparently unaddressed product gaps — meaning the Series B proposal is asking for $40M to fund a strategy the company's own pilots have not yet validated.
3. The unit economics of the sales-led acquisition motion are deteriorating to the point where the model may be structurally unviable in the current competitive environment: rising CAC, falling win rates, and a 19-month payback period against a 14-month runway is an acute operating constraint.

### Open Questions

The following questions must be answered before committing to any capital strategy:

- What fraction of the rising gross logo churn is directly attributable to the PE competitor versus other causes (product dissatisfaction, practice closures, pricing sensitivity)? This determines whether the core threat is existential or manageable.
- What is the engineering scope (cost, timeline) to close the documented DSO product gaps (multi-location reporting, SSO)? Two failed pilots name the same gaps — is this a 3-month build or an 18-month platform rewrite?
- Is the DSO segment genuinely underserved, or is there an entrenched enterprise dental software incumbent already capturing that market? Meridian's failed pilots do not reveal the competitive landscape in DSOs.
- Is there an expansion-revenue opportunity in the independent practice segment — add-on modules, usage-based pricing, or workflow depth — that could rebuild NRR above 100% without requiring the upmarket move?
- What is the PE competitor's current coverage? How many of Meridian's 2,400 customers have already been approached or converted, and what is the forward trajectory of that rollout?

## 2. Growth Barrier Diagnosis

*This section isolates the constraint that actually binds — not a list of initiatives.*

### Growth Gap

To sustain a growth trajectory that supports a Series B valuation at a reasonable multiple, Meridian needs YoY growth in the 40–50% range — approximately double the current 28% and against a decelerating trend. At the current pace of deceleration (roughly 15–20 percentage points of growth lost per year), Meridian reaches sub-10% growth within 2–3 years. The gap is not a pipeline shortfall addressable by adding AEs — the growth equation is breaking at acquisition, retention, and expansion simultaneously.

### Driver Tree

The SaaS growth equation: **Net New ARR = (Qualified Pipeline × Win Rate × ACV) + Expansion ARR − Churned ARR**

Breaking down each driver:

- **Qualified Pipeline**: CAC up 38% means generating the same pipeline now costs 38% more capital, or the pipeline volume has declined. Either way, the acquisition funnel is more expensive per won dollar.
- **Win Rate** (32% → 24%): A 25% relative decline. This almost certainly reflects the free-competitor effect — sales execution does not decline this fast absent a structural market shift.
- **ACV** (~$5,900): No evidence of growth. The independent practice segment has limited willingness or ability to pay for premium tiers, and Meridian appears to have no packaging for expansion revenue.
- **Expansion ARR**: NRR declined from 112% to 96% — expansion has effectively collapsed. No upsell motion or add-on module evidence is present in the inputs.
- **Churned ARR**: Gross logo churn at 14% and rising. At 2,400 customers and $5,900 ACV, this is roughly $2M+ in ARR churned annually and growing.
- **Sales Capacity**: 18 AEs, sales-led. Capacity is not the binding bottleneck — efficiency is. Adding AEs into a deteriorating win rate and rising CAC compounds the capital problem.

The growth formula is breaking at three simultaneous points: acquisition effectiveness (win rate), acquisition cost (CAC), and retention/expansion (NRR). A business with one broken driver can repair it operationally. Three simultaneous failures indicate a structural market position problem, not an execution problem.

### Barrier Assessment

| Driver | Evidence | Impact | Confidence | Root Cause vs Symptom |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Win Rate Decline | 32% → 24% in 12 months; PE competitor offering a free product bundled to dental insurance | High — each percentage point of win rate across 18 AEs represents material lost ARR; compounded with rising CAC, this is a capital-efficiency multiplier | High | Root cause: free competitor has structurally eliminated price as a competitive lever in the independent practice segment — win rate loss is the symptom of an unwinnable price-competitive dynamic |
| NRR Collapse | 112% → 96% over 18 months; gross logo churn rising; no expansion module evidence | High — below 100% NRR transforms the customer base from a growth engine into a liability; recovery requires both churn reduction and expansion revenue, neither of which is evidenced | High | Root cause: no expansion packaging (ACV flat, no add-ons) combined with competitive churn from free substitute — this is a product strategy gap, not a customer success execution gap |
| CAC Inflation | CAC up 38% in 12 months; payback 19 months against 14-month runway | Medium-High — not yet destroying value in isolation, but trajectory is unsustainable and the payback horizon exceeds current runway | Medium | Symptom of win rate decline (same pipeline cost, fewer wins distributed across a smaller win pool) compounded by potential independent practice market saturation in Meridian's addressable geography |
| DSO Product Gaps | Two churned pilots; both citing same gaps: multi-location reporting + SSO | Critical for the Series B thesis — these gaps block the only proposed escape route from the core segment deterioration | High | Root cause: product was architected for single-location independent practices and lacks multi-tenancy / enterprise-grade identity management — not a feature gap, a foundational product architecture gap |
| Segment Concentration | 2,400 customers, almost all 1–3 chair practices; single-segment revenue base | Medium — concentration amplifies the competitive risk; the PE competitor does not need to win the entire market to critically damage Meridian's growth | Medium | Root cause: ICP defined narrowly and product built exclusively for that ICP; no adjacent segment has been productized |

### Binding Constraint

**The binding constraint is competitive free substitution in the core segment, compounded by a product that is architecturally unqualified for the only adjacent segment with sufficient ACV to offset the core erosion.**

More precisely: the PE competitor's bundling strategy has removed price from the buying decision for independent practices, which is simultaneously driving win rate decline and accelerating churn. The natural escape route — moving upmarket to DSOs, which have higher ACV, multi-year contracts, and are not yet targeted by the free-bundling strategy — requires multi-location reporting and SSO capabilities that two real-world pilots have confirmed are absent. Meridian is caught between a deteriorating core it cannot defend at current price levels and an escape route it cannot yet qualify for — with 14 months of runway as the clock.

### Recommended Actions

Each action traces directly to the binding constraint:

1. **DSO product gap sprint (immediate)**: Commission engineering scoping for multi-location reporting and SSO before any enterprise hiring or capital deployment. If the product gaps require more than 12 months to close, the $40M Series B timeline is broken and should not be pursued. The actions under the Series B thesis are sequenced wrong — product must precede enterprise GTM.
2. **Competitive churn forensics (30 days)**: Segment the 2,400-customer base by churn risk and attribute gross logo churn to the PE competitor specifically. If more than 50% of rising churn is competitive, the core defense window is closing faster than the upmarket timeline allows — and the strategy must account for the core declining to $10–11M ARR before DSO revenue materializes.
3. **Expansion revenue packaging (parallel)**: Design 1–2 add-on modules (e.g., patient communications automation, billing analytics, insurance verification) targeting NRR recovery above 105% in the independent practice segment. Rebuilding NRR extends runway and improves the growth profile for any fundraise, regardless of which strategic path is chosen.

## 3. Assumption Audit

*This section surfaces the load-bearing beliefs underlying the proposed strategy and converts the weak ones into testable hypotheses.*

### Strategy Being Tested

Raise a $40M Series B to fund an upmarket move into DSOs: build multi-location enterprise product capabilities, hire an enterprise sales team, and capture DSO accounts at materially higher ACV than the current independent practice base, with the expectation that DSO ARR will offset and exceed the deterioration in the core segment.

### Assumption Register

| Assumption | Category | Importance | Evidence Strength | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DSOs represent an accessible, underserved market Meridian can realistically win | Market | Critical | Weak — no successful pilot; no market sizing provided; two failed attempts are the only direct evidence | High: if DSOs are already effectively served by an entrenched enterprise dental platform, the upmarket move enters a two-front war with no home base |
| The documented product gaps (multi-location reporting, SSO) are buildable within a 12-month window and reasonable capital envelope | Operational | Critical | Weak — no engineering estimate available; the fact that two churned pilots cited the same gaps without subsequent product advancement suggests underestimated complexity | High: if the build takes 18+ months or requires a foundational platform rewrite, the Series B capital will be consumed before a single DSO contract can be signed |
| Meridian's existing brand, relationships, and go-to-market motion are transferable to DSO buyers | Organizational | High | Weak — DSO procurement is typically centralized, IT-involved, multi-stakeholder, and on 6–18 month cycles, not practice-manager decisions like the independent practice motion | Medium: the entire existing sales team and playbook may need to be replaced for enterprise DSO selling |
| The independent practice base will remain stable enough to fund operations during the DSO product build and GTM ramp | Market | High | Low — NRR is 96% and falling; gross logo churn is 14% and rising; the PE competitor's rollout trajectory is unknown but assumed ongoing | High: if core ARR declines faster than assumed, the runway assumption breaks before DSO revenue materializes |
| $40M is sufficient capital to fund the product build, enterprise sales team ramp, and transition period | Economic | High | Unknown — no financial model or enterprise sales ramp assumption provided; standard enterprise AE ramp is 12–18 months to quota | Medium-High: if product takes 12 months and enterprise AEs take 15 months to ramp, the raise may be consumed before a meaningful DSO ARR base exists |
| No well-capitalized incumbent already serves the DSO segment with switching costs that Meridian cannot overcome | Competitive | High | Unknown — not addressed in the inputs | Medium: entrenched DSO software incumbents (if they exist) would have the same structural advantage over Meridian in DSOs that the PE competitor has in independent practices |
| The PE competitor's free-bundling strategy will not extend upmarket to DSOs, preserving DSOs as a clean market for Meridian | Competitive | Medium | Unknown — PE-backed business with dental insurance cross-subsidy could rationally expand the bundling strategy to DSOs if unit economics support it | Medium: if the PE competitor moves upmarket simultaneously, Meridian faces the same dynamics at a higher cost of sale |

### Load-Bearing Assumptions

1. **The DSO segment is addressable and closable within the capital envelope.** This assumption carries the entire Series B logic: the raise makes sense only if DSO is a genuinely open and winnable market, the documented product gaps are closable within 12 months and a bounded budget, and the enterprise sales motion can ramp before the capital is exhausted. Two churned pilots, zero evidence of market sizing, and no engineering estimate for the product gaps mean this assumption is currently unverified, high-importance, and high-risk. If this assumption fails, the $40M raise depletes cash while Meridian also loses the core segment.

2. **The independent practice base will hold long enough to fund the transition.** The upmarket strategy implicitly assumes the core segment continues generating the revenue and operational stability that supports operations while DSO product is built and DSO sales ramp. Given NRR at 96% and falling, churn at 14% and rising, and an active free-competitor rollout of unknown but presumably growing scope, this assumption is under live stress. A scenario where core ARR declines from $14.2M to $10–11M over 18 months — entirely plausible at current trajectory — would substantially change the risk calculus of a $40M raise.

### Test Plan

| Assumption | Test | Data Needed | Owner | Decision Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DSO product gaps can be closed within 12 months at reasonable cost | CTO commissions a 30-day engineering scoping study of multi-location reporting and SSO; run a co-development session with one willing DSO to identify the exact feature requirements | Engineering architecture assessment; time and cost estimate to production-ready; DSO design partner letter of intent | CTO + Head of Product | If engineering scope exceeds 12 months or $4M to production-ready feature set, the Series B timeline as proposed is broken — the board decision should shift to a smaller bridge raise ($8–12M) focused on closing product gaps and defending core before re-assessing the upmarket thesis |
| DSO is a winnable, underserved market with favorable economics | Conduct structured discovery interviews with 15–20 DSO operators (5–50 location groups) across three geographies; benchmark the top two DSO-focused competitors on features, pricing, and switching costs | DSO buyer interview transcripts with stated pain points and current vendor satisfaction; competitive feature-gap analysis; addressable DSO market size estimate | VP Sales + VP Product (90 days) | If fewer than one-third of interviewed DSOs are actively seeking a new vendor or report dissatisfaction with their current solution, the DSO market opportunity is not near-term accessible at the capital scale proposed — revise thesis to a 3–5 year horizon or deprioritize |
| Core base will hold during transition (runway assumption integrity check) | Segment all 2,400 customers by churn risk score; build a 24-month ARR model under three scenarios: current-trajectory (14% churn), accelerated (20% churn), and defended (10% churn) | Customer health scores; competitive win/loss attribution data; CRM churn reason codes for the last 12 months | VP Customer Success + CFO (45 days) | If base-case 24-month model shows core ARR declining below $11M before DSO ARR can realistically reach $3M, the Series B should not be structured as an upmarket bet — restructure as a defensive raise to extend runway and fix unit economics in the core first |

## 4. Framed Recommendation

**Verdict:** Test first

Meridian should not raise a $40M Series B to fund the upmarket DSO move as currently framed. The evidence from two churned pilots, documented product gaps that have not been addressed, and a 14-month runway make the raise a bet on a hypothesis the company's own operating history currently contradicts. However, the status quo is equally indefensible: the core segment faces structural competitive pressure from a free substitute, and the growth trajectory reaches stall speed within 2–3 years without a strategic pivot. The disciplined path is a 90-day evidence sprint before committing capital at Series B scale: (1) engineering-scope the DSO product gaps to determine if they are closable within 12 months and a bounded budget, (2) conduct structured market discovery with 15–20 DSO buyers to validate the segment is genuinely winnable and underserved, and (3) model the core ARR trajectory under stress scenarios to confirm the funding runway assumption. If those tests return affirmative results — DSO gaps closable in under 12 months, at least one-third of DSO prospects actively seeking a new solution — the appropriate raise is a $12–18M bridge to fund a focused DSO MVP and a limited enterprise pilot, not a $40M full-transformation raise. The $40M figure belongs on the table only after Meridian has a signed DSO contract on a product that has closed the documented gaps. The answer to the board decision is: the $40M Series B is premature by at least one evidence cycle; the board is being asked to price a bet that has not yet been derisked enough to size.
